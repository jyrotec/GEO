# GEO
An attempt at a comprehensive Geographic Organization of Earth, or GEO

Overview:

As important as borders are to our world, more often than not, they aren’t drawn with accuracy with respect to the people or typography that they mean to represent. Think about the historical Scramble for Africa, the majority of colonial borders, or even the modern nation state models that many political entities today follow. These ignore the dynamic and ever changing realities of anthropological and geographic borders, which are often non rigid and exist cleaner on a spectrum rather than discretely. By considering numerous variables, such as the individual and unique geography, hydrology and the cultures that develop around them, as well as linguistics and general modern demographics, the Geographic Organization of Earth model, or GEO, was developed as a framework to approach borders with a different perspective. Instead of focusing solely on administrative practicality and realism, it instead prioritizes unitized divisions of land that share some commonality. This could be something like:

- a river valley with similar or cohesive cultural mapping;

- a larger multicultural metropolitan region whose diversity creates cohesion; or

- a vast conservation zone that defines the typography.

Removing the strict need for political realism allows the framework to act like holistically and without partiality or nationalism in representation. (Though the model does tend to favour some political dimension through its division system) Basically, although the purpose is to map cohesive anthropological regions in parallel with geographic, not only is there immense overlap but the model tends towards a geographic representative system since demographics are very dynamic. The model in no way attempts to perfectly map every culture, region, dialect, or unique geographic point of interest and there will be imperfections. The overall attempt is to model the world more realistically than many modern entities do today.

Divisions:

GEO utilizes multiple different types of divisions to explain the regions it represents. All divisions are known as “unit states,” which are meant to be continuous plots of land that have something in common. There are multiple different types of states, such as:

Independent Unit States: A simple unit state functioning as described above. To be an independent unit state, generally the population and economic status is considered and given it meets the appropriate criteria, which is to be discussed, it will qualify.

United Unit States: Two or more unit states that could function independently based upon their individual population, geography and independence but instead function together through a shared identity.

Metropolitan United Unit States: Two or more unit states that are all tethered to a central metropolitan core, where the tethered states are also metropolitan, suburban, industrial, or heavy developmental regions.

Independent state criteria:

- a population of at minimum 15,000

- economic independence (such as forestry, agriculture, industrial, etc.)

When a state doesn’t meet such criteria, it is considered a Semi-Autonomous State, which as mentioned would be represented via proxy by another neighbouring state of independent status.

There are also other states that rely on others by proxy. These include:

Wilderness States: unpopulated divisions that exist to represent land independent of settlement

Natural Conservation States: similar to wilderness divisions but these retain special status as presently existing conservation areas

Cultural Heritage States: these represent territories of indigenous peoples and communities that would otherwise be clumped together with their surrounding states, which is done to preserve their independence and territorial status
